My Design Process

I‚Äôm frequently asked to solve problems in areas I‚Äôm unfamiliar with.

My job has always been to build a bridge between the needs of the stakeholder and the needs of the user. Ideally, but seldom a linear process!

Defining the Problem

When starting on a new design project I need to understand what the stakeholder wants to accomplish. We define and agree on the core problem that needs to be solved through a variety of methods.

Understanding the Problem Space

I spend time learning the ins-and-outs of the domain that I‚Äôm working in. Important when joining a new team or working in an industry that I have no contextual understanding of.

If I have the chance to talk to users, this research helps set my conversations up for success

Gathering User Needs

Depending on the scope of the project, I‚Äôll look to interview actual users. I‚Äôll prepare a thoughtful interview to validate the problem and understand how the target user group expects to have their needs met v.s. how the stakeholder intends to.

Prototypes and Concepts

Low-risk way to test various hypothetical solutions to the problems that may have been clarified at this point. User and or stakeholder feedback at this stage is valuable for determining my direction from here, whether that means to circle back or to keep moving!

trata creates a SaaS platform that addresses the financial planning, decision support and continuous cost improvement needs of healthcare providers. As a member of the 3 person Technical Communication team at Strata Decision Technology, I took up the initiative to design, develop, and update the information hierarchy of the product Help Center.

Due to NDA, I‚Äôm unable to disclose the specific data points and numbers related to the results of my work. As well as the initial version. I'll provide a mock-up representation.

The Strata Platform is a large web application spanning 3 total solutions, each with 4 modules and a variety of general security and dashboard features. Any healthcare provider can have a variety of mix-and-matched solutions and modules based on their needs, and the Help Center contains thousands of pages of supporting help documentation to help the users with nearly any task in the product.

In addition, the Help Center is accessible from nearly any point within the application via a help center icon.

Because a user landing on the help page could be using any variety of solutions and modules, and the fact that the Help Center is accessible from nearly any context within the application means that the Help Center needs to be designed in a way to help all users, at any time, from any point.

Within a small team of 3, I didn‚Äôt have the technical resources to build a solution that would detect and display only the help pages relevant to the parts of the product that customers licensed. This would have to function as a catch-all.

Data First

As a member of the documentation team, and also because this was my own internal initiative run by myself, I didn‚Äôt have direct access to users to help inform my design decisions for the Help Center. Instead, I relied internally on three main sources of information:

  1. Product Managers
  2. Technical Support Staff
  3. Historical Google Analytics Data

Before building anything, I started first by looking at the usage trends after plugging Google analytics into the existing documentation pages and allowing it to collect data for about a month. I learned a few key things from this:

  1. Most users prefer to use the search bar to find help topics relative to their problem
  2. A minority, but growing percentage of users were using the Help Center on both tablet and phones, in that order
  3. PDF guides that we spend a lot of time developing are not very popular

In addition to using analytics, I held SME (Subject Matter Expert) interviews with a few of the Technical Support staff. Because I‚Äôm designing to help users find answers to their problems, I wanted to see what kinds of support problems users most frequently had. I found three things from this:

Without direct access to users, I held conversations with Product Managers from each of the primary product categories to determine the most logical layout of the clickable solutions within the Help Center landing page.

Putting it Together

Based on all my prior work of analytics, and working with both Technical Support and Product, built the layout of the help center in three primary sections:

Help Center Search Bar

Analytics show the Help Center Search bar is the primary thing users click when landing on the Help Center for the first time. I made this front-and-center.

Browse by Solution

Browse by Solution allows users to visually navigate a display of information based on categories I made with Product Managers input.

Additional Resources

The Additional Resources displays tertiary information: the technical guides that clients routinely request from technical support are now permanently at home here, allowing clients to regularly use the Help Center for this information rather than calling support.

Visually, the Help Center needed to have restraint. The focus of this page is to help users find an answer to their problem and not get in their way while still matching the overall company brand.

The colors and layout were chosen to match the Strata brand. I used Foundation framework for the front-end enabling it to be fully responsive across screen sizes that I learned were in use from Google Analytics.

Closing Thoughts

I learned a lot about keeping design down to realistic constraints during this project. The users were all healthcare workers in hospital or office settings, so the branding needed to fit these users' expectations without getting in their way of using the Help Center.

In many companies, there's a perception that design is all about aesthetics. Aesthetics are important, but there‚Äôs also a lot of logic and reasoning that‚Äôs behind a lot of design decisions, and you have to be able to justify why you made the design decisions you did.
