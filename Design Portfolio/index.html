My Design Process

I’m frequently asked to solve problems in areas I’m unfamiliar with.

My job has always been to build a bridge between the needs of the stakeholder and the needs of the user. Ideally, but seldom a linear process!

Defining the Problem

When starting on a new design project I need to understand what the stakeholder wants to accomplish. We define and agree on the core problem that needs to be solved through a variety of methods.

Understanding the Problem Space

I spend time learning the ins-and-outs of the domain that I’m working in. Important when joining a new team or working in an industry that I have no contextual understanding of.

If I have the chance to talk to users, this research helps set my conversations up for success

Gathering User Needs

Depending on the scope of the project, I’ll look to interview actual users. I’ll prepare a thoughtful interview to validate the problem and understand how the target user group expects to have their needs met v.s. how the stakeholder intends to.

Prototypes and Concepts

Low-risk way to test various hypothetical solutions to the problems that may have been clarified at this point. User and or stakeholder feedback at this stage is valuable for determining my direction from here, whether that means to circle back or to keep moving!

Updating the product
Help Center.

The Brief:

I created the visual brand and information architecture of the product Help Center used by some of the largest healthcare providers across North America while keeping it fully responsive for both tablet and desktop use.

My Role:

UX/UI Designer
Information Architect

Results:

Increased customer usage of Help Center
Installed analytics into documentation system

1.5 months
1 person project

trata creates a SaaS platform that addresses the financial planning, decision support and continuous cost improvement needs of healthcare providers. As a member of the 3 person Technical Communication team at Strata Decision Technology, I took up the initiative to design, develop, and update the information hierarchy of the product Help Center.

Due to NDA, I’m unable to disclose the specific data points and numbers related to the results of my work. As well as the initial version. I'll provide a mock-up representation.

The Strata Platform is a large web application spanning 3 total solutions, each with 4 modules and a variety of general security and dashboard features. Any healthcare provider can have a variety of mix-and-matched solutions and modules based on their needs, and the Help Center contains thousands of pages of supporting help documentation to help the users with nearly any task in the product.

In addition, the Help Center is accessible from nearly any point within the application via a help center icon.

Because a user landing on the help page could be using any variety of solutions and modules, and the fact that the Help Center is accessible from nearly any context within the application means that the Help Center needs to be designed in a way to help all users, at any time, from any point.

Within a small team of 3, I didn’t have the technical resources to build a solution that would detect and display only the help pages relevant to the parts of the product that customers licensed. This would have to function as a catch-all.

Data First

As a member of the documentation team, and also because this was my own internal initiative run by myself, I didn’t have direct access to users to help inform my design decisions for the Help Center. Instead, I relied internally on three main sources of information:

  1. Product Managers
  2. Technical Support Staff
  3. Historical Google Analytics Data

Before building anything, I started first by looking at the usage trends after plugging Google analytics into the existing documentation pages and allowing it to collect data for about a month. I learned a few key things from this:

  1. Most users prefer to use the search bar to find help topics relative to their problem
  2. A minority, but growing percentage of users were using the Help Center on both tablet and phones, in that order
  3. PDF guides that we spend a lot of time developing are not very popular

In addition to using analytics, I held SME (Subject Matter Expert) interviews with a few of the Technical Support staff. Because I’m designing to help users find answers to their problems, I wanted to see what kinds of support problems users most frequently had. I found three things from this:

  1. Product Managers
  2. Technical Support Staff
  3. Historical Google Analytics Data

Without direct access to users, I held conversations with Product Managers from each of the primary product categories to determine the most logical layout of the clickable solutions within the Help Center landing page.

Putting it Together

Based on all my prior work of analytics, and working with both Technical Support and Product, built the layout of the help center in three primary sections:

Help Center Search Bar

Analytics show the Help Center Search bar is the primary thing users click when landing on the Help Center for the first time. I made this front-and-center.

Browse by Solution

Browse by Solution allows users to visually navigate a display of information based on categories I made with Product Managers input.

Additional Resources

The Additional Resources displays tertiary information: the technical guides that clients routinely request from technical support are now permanently at home here, allowing clients to regularly use the Help Center for this information rather than calling support.

Visually, the Help Center needed to have restraint. The focus of this page is to help users find an answer to their problem and not get in their way while still matching the overall company brand.

The colors and layout were chosen to match the Strata brand. I used Foundation framework for the front-end enabling it to be fully responsive across screen sizes that I learned were in use from Google Analytics.

Closing Thoughts

I learned a lot about keeping design down to realistic constraints during this project. The users were all healthcare workers in hospital or office settings, so the branding needed to fit these users' expectations without getting in their way of using the Help Center.

In many companies, there's a perception that design is all about aesthetics. Aesthetics are important, but there’s also a lot of logic and reasoning that’s behind a lot of design decisions, and you have to be able to justify why you made the design decisions you did.

I had no direct access to users for this project, so I needed to rely on getting input primarily from Google Analytics and the various teams I interviewed throughout the company. As a member of the documentation team, and not the design team (at this time) I could have brought more confidence and made a stronger push for interviewing and testing users directly.

March, 2020 - present
3 person project

This was an entirely remote project. I worked among a team of designers across 2 primary stages of this project across several months, primarily within a UX role. Between the two stages, a UI designer stepped in to define the style and establish an Invision Design System that I worked within and maintained for the duration of the project.

My primary contribution was near the end, where I was asked to lead the effort of quantifying and validating the value of a new subscription feature to the app, called The Watchdog.

iwa offers in-home hydroponic gardening systems that are self-contained and self-regulated. The Niwa One hydroponics pod automatically monitors and changes the growing conditions of plants within its enclosure, providing a system tailored to all levels of gardening experience.

Niwa is expanding its product line and adding a new product to the mix: an IoT connected device called The Niwa Smart Strip targeted to DIY growers. This wi-fi smart strip is accompanied by a new mobile app meant to configure, control, and monitor all components of the smart strip. Users can plug in their own devices like lights, a heater, or water pump into the Niwa Smart Strip to ensure their plant is getting what it needs to successfully grow.

Creating the Watchdog

The Niwa stakeholders had a potential monetization feature of the app, called The Watchdog. The Watchdog is meant to be a collection of features built into the app and that automatically protects the crops while providing notifications and alerts to the user. The customer should identify this as he's investing into a "watch dog" constantly watching the crop for them. I was asked to explore the business feasibility of the Niwa watchdog feature as well as determine and test how this new feature could fit into the current specifications of the current mobile app.

A lot of previous design work had already been accomplished. Our UX team had already established a user flow to the DIY growing challenge, and our UI designer had created a Design System that made it easy for us to quickly adhere our design solutions to a guide.

There were a lot of questions I needed to answer in a short amount of time and wrapping up this project before the intended launch date:

Do users see value in automatic alerts?

Can we charge for this, and if so, how much?

How does Watchdog fit into the established user flow?

Do users even want this?

Starting the Research

We had a great guideline for developing user research questions, such as in our approach to the initial exploratory/foundational interviews that happened before this Watchdog project. But, for developing this new feature it was important not let users know what we were thinking of building.

We didn’t want to give them any leading questions or leading bias, invalidating our goal. Once they either arrived at the conclusions themselves or brought up the feature, we then felt comfortable asking them our more pointed questions.

We created a script that was very conceptual, intentionally just a little vague. This helped keep test users from being biased, and allowed us to “take” ideas from them as they thought out loud.. The hydroponics users knew better than us what their needs are, and they knew better the contexts in which they might want to use their new app and this new feature.

Designing a mobile
Travel App.

The Brief:

This is a student project. Starting with a project brief, I created an entire mobile-app prototype including the site-map, and user flow. I've focused this on the UI and visual aesthetic.

My Role:

UX/UI Designer
Visual Designer

Results:

Created UI elements and visual identity

6 weeks
1 person project

ripCrew is a mobile app that provides information and recommendations about where people can go and what to see in different cities around the world. When designing this app, I applied user research to determine what the product should do and how it should do it while practicing user-centered design to create the best experience possible based on user need.

I started this project with a focus on functionality and creating a solution based on user need, then focused heavily on the visual UI and branding component.

To better understand the travel space, I started with research to better understand the product space, competitors, and industry trends.

I also researched the viability of a mobile-app-only approach as the project brief indicated that this would be the most affordable product to build for the company. As part of my competitive analysis, I researched 3 primary competitors that specialize in the mobile travel space.

I assessed the strengths and weaknesses of current and potential competitors to gain a better understanding of the product market, and determine opportunities to differentiate. I also wanted to learn about what is working and isn’t working well within similar products.

“My goal was to identify the competitors and their strategies to determine their strengths and weaknesses relative to what TripCrew wanted to be.”

Visual Competitive Analysis

After identifying the primary competitors and their target audiences, I needed to document the look and feel of each primary competitor to get an understanding of how both colors, typography, and information architecture appeals to their users in the travel space.

Some of the questions I asked myself:

  • Find out how a competitor’s onboarding is structured visually and how they persuade users to sign up and make use of their offerings.
  • What color scheme are they using? What could be the reasons behind their color decisions?
  • What audience does the brand speak to in particular?
  • What is the look and feel? Modern? High end? Why or why not?

Sitemapping

After comparing the sitemaps of the primary competitors, I had a better idea of how various IA patterns work across both mobile devices and in the mobile travel space.

I created this basic sitemap to help set and guide the fundamental IA and basic user-flow of what the TripCrew mobile application may be. This document continued to serve as a moving target throughout the entire design process as I received input from both users and stakeholders throughout the process.

Moodboarding

Using the visual competitive analysis as a starting point to inform myself of what colors (and even shapes) are used within the mobile-travel space, I created and tested two intentionally divergent moodboards--an arrangement of images, colors, etc. intended to evoke or project a particular style or concept.

Moodboarding cont.

Ultimately, users favored the comfortable rounded feel of the rounded moodboard, but the bold colors of the sharper one.

Based primarily on user feedback from the target audience, I combined both the rounded feel and the bold hues to drive the overall visual style of the app:

Closing Thoughts

I grew immensely as a designer during this project--this was the first mobile application that I ever worked on as a designer. An added challenge was the fact that I needed to draft and create the entire experience from the ground up, with nothing but a project brief to start from and the help of a mentor.

Branding, moodboarding, creating and testing user flows, “identifying the problem space” were all concepts I was familiar with going into this project. The “UX process” and associated buzzwords was something that had been drilled into my head so much, that I was convinced that for this project it would maybe be largely superfluous if I just “did whatever needed to be done”. But on more than one occasion, I found myself needing to backtrack and review why I made certain decisions when questioned. Without having documented my moves and conclusions, or even moving too quickly through certain steps (defining clearly the problem(s) that needed to be solved), I often needed to backtrack and re-do certain parts of the process, adding hours to my time.

In conclusion I realized that my design process itself needed to have a flexible balance between both process-following and a nimble DIY approach.

ontractPodAi, a London-based startup developed what is describes as an AI-powered contract lifecycle management software. Founded in 2012, ContractPodAi offers an “end-to-end” solution spanning three main aspects of contract management: contract generation, repository, and third-party review.

Its AI offering, which uses IBM’s Watson, is meant to streamline the contract management process and reduce the burden on corporate in-house legal teams.

Target Audience:

B2B -- large firms that use contracts on a regular basis.

Purpose:

Generate initial interest, establish confidence in the brand and guide the potential customer to eventually convert as a contact or sale. The two primary user goals are: downloading brochure + booking demos.

I researched common web information structures to determine which best meets the goal of the client: Guide a business user to eventually convert as a contact or sale

Marketing:

Should be a fair amount of contrast between the levels of information. The high contrast will help guide users through my narrative focusing them on the most important ideas first: Exactly what ContractPodAi needs.

Blog/Info:

A medium information ratio will still provide a nice contrast between the levels of type but help reduce the length of a page. With less contrast the article’s title will be smaller, which enables fitting more words on a line and reducing line breaks: Maybe useful deeper in the sales funnel, but not what I needed here.

Product:

You will want a variety of information sizes. Needs to flexible and allow for a variety of content but also versatile enough to support smaller type needs like input titles: This is not the product itself and not what I need.

First attempt and final attempt
